Product Discovery Lessons from IMTEX: What Manufacturing Customers Actually Care About

Recently, I had the opportunity to explore IMTEX, one of India's largest industrial manufacturing exhibitions.

As a Mechanical Engineering student with a growing interest in product management and business strategy, I walked into the event expecting to learn about machines, automation systems, and manufacturing technologies.

I did learn about those things. But the biggest lessons had nothing to do with machinery.

They were lessons about customers.

Everyone Loves Features. Customers Love Outcomes.

As I moved from one booth to another, I noticed something interesting. Exhibitors were eager to explain technical specifications. Customers were asking completely different questions.

Instead of asking: "What technology powers this machine?" they were asking:

  • How much downtime will this reduce?
  • What's the maintenance cost?
  • How quickly can operators learn it?
  • How long before we see ROI?

The contrast was striking. Companies were talking about features. Customers were talking about business outcomes. That distinction sits at the heart of product discovery.

The Simplest Solutions Attracted The Most Attention

Another pattern became impossible to ignore. The products attracting the most interest weren't necessarily the most advanced. They were often the easiest to understand.

Whether it was machine interfaces, monitoring dashboards, or industrial software, simplicity consistently stood out. People don't want complexity; they want clarity. This is true whether you're building industrial machinery, SaaS products, or AI tools.

Good products reduce cognitive load. They don't increase it.

Observation Beats Assumption

One of the most valuable experiences was simply observing conversations. When engineers design products internally, it's easy to assume we understand user needs. Reality is usually more nuanced.

Customers often care about things that product teams never discuss. Sometimes a seemingly minor workflow issue becomes a major purchasing factor. Other times, a feature that seemed revolutionary barely influences decision-making.

These observations reinforced something I've been learning repeatedly:

You cannot discover customer needs from a conference room. You have to meet customers where they are.

What This Taught Me About Product Thinking

The event reminded me that product development is not primarily a technology challenge. It's a problem-understanding challenge.

Technology matters. Engineering matters. Execution matters. But all of those things come after understanding the customer.

The best products aren't built by asking: "What can we build?" They're built by asking: "What problem is worth solving?"

Final Thoughts

I attended IMTEX expecting to learn about manufacturing innovation. I left with a deeper appreciation for customer-centric thinking.

Whether you're building industrial equipment, AI products, or consumer applications, the principle remains the same: Customers don't buy products. They buy better outcomes.
